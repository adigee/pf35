The Decision
Module

Sr. Product Designer

Interaction Design AI / ML Operations

Unbabel's AI quality estimation model needed specialist engineers to operate it — a bottleneck that didn't scale. The brief was to put that control in the hands of the ops team: making the model's reasoning legible without making it overwhelming.

Context

An AI pipeline with a human in the loop

Unbabel combines machine translation with human post-editing to produce high-quality translations at scale. At the centre of this pipeline sits the Quality Estimation (QE) model — an AI system that scores every translated segment and decides whether it needs a human editor to review it, or whether it's good enough to pass directly to the customer.

That routing decision is everything. Send too much to human review and turnaround slows; send too little and quality drops. Getting the threshold right — and keeping it right across hundreds of customer pipelines — was the operational challenge the team faced every day.

Problem

The model worked. The interface didn't.

Configuring QE thresholds required engineering access. Every adjustment — a new customer profile, a language-pair tweak, a sensitivity change — went through a ticket to the ML team and waited in a queue. The ops team had the domain knowledge to make these calls confidently, but no way to act on it.

The user problem and the business goal pointed at the same solution: surface QE as a lever that the ops team could operate directly, without engineering involvement.

Understanding the model

Decision making based on a threshold

The QE model produces two types of scores: a segment-level score for each translated sentence, and a document-level score that aggregates across the whole job. When a score falls below a set threshold, the segment is routed to a human editor. Above the threshold, it skips review entirely.

The model also learns continuously — human edits feed back as annotations that improve future scoring. This meant that how ops team members set thresholds today would shape what the model learned tomorrow. That feedback loop made the design challenge more significant, not less.

Starting point

Numeric controls nobody used

The existing Decision Module was a WHEN / IS / THEN conditional logic form: raw threshold inputs presented as bare number fields. It was built for engineers, and it showed. Analytics confirmed what we already suspected — the numeric QE controls were not being used. The interface assumed a mental model that ops team members simply didn't have.

Discovery sessions revealed the core gap: ops team members understood the outcome they wanted (fewer reviews for high-trust language pairs, tighter scrutiny for sensitive customers) but had no framework for translating that intent into a numeric threshold. The abstraction was wrong.

The design pivot

From thresholds to profiles

The key insight from research was that ops team members thought in postures, not numbers. "We want to be conservative here" or "this customer trusts our MT quality" — qualitative stances that implied a direction on a spectrum.

This led to the introduction of QE Profiles: a named spectrum from Very Conservative to Very Aggressive, each mapping to a threshold range behind the scenes. Choosing a profile meant choosing how much of the translation volume would skip human review — a relationship the ops team could reason about intuitively.

Exploration

Three directions, one constraint

In the divergent phase we explored three concepts, each foregrounding a different model of control. The first let users select a QE profile directly — choosing a qualitative posture. The second let users target a skip volume — expressing intent as a percentage of translations to pass without review. The third focused on quality indication — letting users specify an acceptable quality floor for skipped jobs.

All three could theoretically work, but concept testing quickly revealed that skip volume resonated most. It gave ops team members a concrete, business-legible number to reason about — "we're skipping 34% of translations" — without requiring them to understand threshold mathematics.

"I don't know what 0.85 means, but I know what 30% fewer reviews means for my team's capacity."

First version

Profiles as levels of skip

Decision Module v1 merged the profile and volume concepts: a 5-level slider (corresponding to the QE profiles) with a live donut chart showing the estimated percentage of translations that would skip review. Selecting a level correlated directly to a skip volume, and a quality indicator showed the estimated quality of the jobs being skipped.

The donut chart gave ops team members a feedback loop — choose a level, see the consequence — without having to reason about thresholds at all. The design made the model's decision legible, not just configurable.

User testing

Two rounds, one critical fix

We ran two rounds of moderated usability testing with five ops team members each. Round one established that the slider model was understood and the donut chart was read correctly. What surfaced as a problem was the profile labels themselves — "Level 1 through 5" carried no semantic weight, and participants were reluctant to move away from the default without understanding what they were moving towards.

Round two introduced named levels ("Very conservative" through "Very aggressive") and set "Balanced" as the preselected default. Confidence scores in post-task questionnaires rose markedly. Participants described the named profiles as giving them "permission to act" — the language matched their mental model well enough that they trusted themselves to make a decision.

Final design

Simple, autosaved, balanced by default

The shipped design distilled the interaction to its essentials. A single slider with five named levels, "Balanced" preselected. A donut chart showing the live skip percentage. A "View quality" toggle to reveal the estimated quality distribution of skipped segments — surfaced on demand rather than always-on, reducing cognitive load for routine adjustments.

All changes autosaved, removing the commit friction that had previously made ops team members hesitant to experiment. The design reframed configuration as an iterative, low-stakes act rather than a consequential one-shot decision.

Named profiles, live skip preview, autosave — all changes low-friction and reversible

Outcomes

An uptick in usage — and in ownership

The clearest signal came from product analytics: usage of the Decision Module spiked sharply after the redesign launched, and sustained. The ops team was adjusting QE settings routinely, without engineering tickets, treating the module as a genuine operational tool rather than a legacy artefact to work around.

More meaningfully, post-launch interviews surfaced a shift in how the team described their relationship to the model. The language changed from "we have to ask engineering to change that" to "we adjusted it last week." The design hadn't just made a task easier — it had transferred a form of operational ownership that hadn't existed before.
