On-demand

How we refactored and expanded On-demand for migrating customers with a new use-case. After the acquisition of Lingo24, the platform needed to support Localisation — a use-case it had never handled before.

Introduction

An invisible translation layer with one customer-facing gap

Unbabel was designed to be an invisible B2B translation layer for customer support. Our integrations seamlessly fit into a customer's existing support CRM to intercept and translate customer support messages.

Translating files on the other hand was only possible via On-demand, a primitive customer facing micro-app that provided for customers to upload simple files and submit them for translation. To support Localisation however, we would need to support for more complex file formats, introduce collaboration workflows, add price estimates and more.

To migrate Lingo24 customers to our platform, we decided to completely overhaul On-demand and add support for the above functionalities.

My role

Solo designer, embedded in the team

My team was responsible developing On-demand. As the solo product designer on the team, I partnered with 2 product managers - one from my team and the other building the underlying file translation API to plan the project, align roadmaps and create a UX research plan to learn about Localisation customer needs.

I had parachuted into this team for this project, so my process involved multiple moments of collaboration, participation, design reviews to earn their trust and create empathy in the team for the migrating customers.

Problem definition

A year of debt and a new customer base to serve

On-demand v1 had a year long backlog of feature requests and bugs. The underlying architecture was cracking under volume of orders, the UX flows were not optimised and table stake features were frequently requested.

More than 90% of existing customer would use Unbabel via integrations. Since On-demand was the only customer facing touchpoint for requesting translations, it become clear that it was the ideal touchpoint for exisitng and new customers to translate files. We brainstormed and planned as a team on how we can rebuild On-demand into a reliable and robust file translation system.

Approach

Two phases: rebuild the foundation, then expand

Our plan was two-fold - release an improved On-demand v2 for existing customers; then evolve into On-demand v3 for migrating localisation customers.

Phase I - On-demand v2

The primary focus of On-demand v2 was to introduce search and filters. Due to unexpected volume of usage, customers was struggling to track, find and retrieve their translation orders. In the background, the engineers planned to refactor and rebuild the entire backend to handle higher volumes and newer file APIs. The design of On-demand v2 was focussed on bringing a higher quality to the existing functionalities.

Phase II - On-demand v3

In parallel, I would run a discovery track to understand what the migrating customers would need. This UX research track informed the conversations on how our platform would handle the localisation use-case. This included evaluating the Lingo24 interface for files and identifying which functionalities could be deprecated. All of these activities would culminate into the design of On-demand v3 which was the first customer facing app on the Unbabel platform to handle complex file translations.

Design iteration 1

Divergent exploration before converging

I had studied the backlog and already identified the primary reported customer needs. I engaged the team to share and brainstorm ideas around exceptional search experiences. This helped me understand where the team was and how far we could go. Including engineers in these activities was crucial in teasing out the technical constraints of our front-end framework and the underlying API.

I created multiple explorations of how we could introduce search functionality to On-demand. Divergent explorations helped the team understand the trade-offs involved in terms of the experience vs the technical constraints.

We eventually decided to invest in building a new component for filters and updating the design system. The existing components in the design system for tables and filters were clunky and lacking in terms of experience. Improving it would raise the UX across the board on the platform.

While developing a new component for the design system involved more cross-functional and alignment and effort, demonstrating the value added across the platform (and multiple micro apps) helped get required buy in from the team to invest in a higher quality experience.

Additionally since the underlying APIs systems were being overhauled, the lifecycle of a file translation order changed as well. This provided us with the perfect opportunity to review and improve the emails being sent to customers.

To wrap up, I also defined the instrumentation plan for us to start embedding sensors in this micro-app to capture usage analytics moving forward.

Design iteration 2

A unified platform for two customer types

To learn about the customers we were going to migrate, I planned and executed a research track to start building our in-house knowledge about localisation.

I started by analysing the existing Lingo24 interface for uploading complex files and used it as a starting point to develop a lo-fi prototype on how On-demand could accommodate it on our platform.

Then, I recruited and moderated sessions with 8 customers who represented more than 70% of the total translation volume of Lingo customers. These sessions had a mixed method structure. I started with exploration of their workflows, to uncover how they worked with the files and what they were trying to achieve. And then ended with evaluation, to gather feedback on my lo-fi prototype.

Using this unconventional approach, I was able to fast track to actionable customer inputs and also help shape how the product org understood this new use-case.

Armed with these insights, I designed the next version of On-demand which was eventually rebranded to Projects.

In essence a project was a group of orders that shared the same translation configurations.

The Projects micro-app supported:

  • Multi-file upload
  • Pre-defined templates for translation orders
  • Laid foundation for more complex functionalities (like price estimates, DTP etc)

Reflections

What the process taught me

The primary challenge in this project was the organisational dynamics. There were a total of 3 teams on this initiative, half of whom came from a newly acquired organisation. Apart from the expected culture clashes, there were rampant and legitimate differences in opinion. To forge a path forward, we needed to overcome the reluctance of trying new approaches to serving these migrating customers.

As the only product designer on this entire initiative (that included over 20 people), my research and design work received a great deal of scrutiny as we navigated the unknown and charted a path forward. My PM and I worked to break the initiative down to smaller and manageable chunks as much as possible, building incremental agreements across teams and driving towards valuable milestones.

In addition, I was dropped into a team that was brand new to me (from linguistic tooling to customer tooling). By creating many demos, checkpoints and holding participatory sessions, I was successful in bringing the entire team on board to build a future forward customer experience for existing and migrating customers!
