Lockers PUDO

How we used continuous discovery to bring cancellation rates down and prove product-market fit for a new use-case at Bounce.

Introduction

A new use-case, a leaking funnel

Bounce built its reputation as the world's leading luggage storage network. Expanding into Lockers PUDO — a pick-up and drop-off use-case — represented a meaningful strategic bet. But the early signals were uncomfortable: customers were booking and then cancelling at a rate that called the entire business case into question.

The team needed to answer one question fast: was this a product problem or a market problem? And the only way to find out was to get into the hands and minds of real users, quickly and repeatedly.

My role

Lead designer, embedded in the growth team

I was the sole product designer on the Lockers PUDO initiative, partnering closely with the product manager and a small engineering team. My remit spanned both the discovery practice — understanding why cancellations were happening — and the design execution required to test hypotheses at speed.

Proving PMF in a nascent use-case meant operating without the safety net of established user patterns. Every design decision had to earn its place in the funnel.

Problem definition

High cancellation rate as the primary signal

The cancellation rate for Lockers PUDO was significantly above the benchmark set by the core luggage storage product. Initial hypotheses from the team ranged from pricing to location coverage to trust. None of them were validated.

Rather than designing to a hypothesis, we needed to surface the real reasons — and do it with enough rigour to confidently redirect the product roadmap. That meant building a discovery practice that could produce actionable signal, fast.

The key question was not "how do we reduce cancellations?" — it was "why is the user deciding to cancel, and at which moment?" Getting to that specificity changed what we designed.

Approach

Continuous discovery as the operating model

I set up a lightweight but structured continuous discovery practice around the PUDO experience. This meant weekly interviews with customers who had recently cancelled, combined with analysis of drop-off points in the booking flow and regular synthesis sessions with the product team.

The goal was not to run a single big research project — it was to build a loop: learn, design, test, repeat. Each cycle produced a sharper picture of where the experience was failing to hold users.

Discovery cadence

Bi-weekly customer interviews with recent cancellations anchored the qualitative track. These were short (25 minutes), structured around the moment of cancellation, and synthesised into a running insight repository shared with the full team.

Funnel instrumentation

On the quantitative side, I worked with engineering to instrument the booking flow and surface where users were dropping out. This gave us the "where" to pair with the "why" from interviews — and helped us prioritise which friction points to address first.

Design iterations

Testing assumptions at the speed of discovery

As insights emerged, I translated them into design hypotheses and tested them in rapid cycles. The PUDO booking flow went through multiple iterations — each one targeted at a specific failure mode uncovered in research.

Iteration 1 — Reducing pre-booking uncertainty

Early interviews revealed that users were uncertain about what would happen with their parcel at the locker location. The information architecture of the booking confirmation flow was redesigned to surface the right reassurances at the right moment — before the user had a reason to doubt.

Iteration 2 — Simplifying the handoff moment

A second cluster of cancellations was happening after booking — users arriving at the locker and not completing the handoff. This led to a redesign of the in-app guidance for the physical interaction, reducing the cognitive load of an unfamiliar process.

Reflections

What proving PMF actually looks like

The most important thing I learned on this project is that proving product-market fit is not a single event — it is a steady accumulation of small confirmations, each won through disciplined discovery and rapid iteration.

The continuous discovery practice gave the team a shared language for talking about user problems without debating opinions. That alignment was as valuable as any individual design improvement. When the data started turning in the right direction, everyone understood why — and that made it durable.

The biggest design lesson was one of restraint: the temptation in a high-pressure PMF context is to redesign everything. The discipline of scoping each iteration tightly to a validated insight — and measuring the outcome before moving on — is what made the work legible to the business.
